1Problem statement & objective

Problem. The current property listing card has a cramped layout and weak information hierarchy. Users can't quickly assess affordability, location fit, or agent trust from the card alone, leading to high adview friction and low lead efficiency.

Hypothesis. If users see better-structured listing information, they'll shortlist faster — increasing engagement with the cards on the listing page.

Objective. Restructure the listing card so key decision-making information (price, location, property type, agent trust signals) is more visible and consistently positioned, helping users evaluate and shortlist properties without having to open multiple adviews.

2Success metrics

Primary

Property card CTR PRIMARY
total_clicks_on_property_card / total_views_on_adlist. Counts each click on a property card (not unique users). Measures whether the redesigned card drives more engagement per listing-page view.

Supporting

Adlist → Adview rate (unique users) SUPPORTING
unique_users_landed_on_adview_from_adlist / total_users_on_adlist. Whether more distinct users reach an adview detail page after the redesign.

Note on leads metrics: Leads per adview is intentionally not a primary or supporting metric here. The card change is upper-funnel and structural — same information, different layout — so we wouldn't expect direct downstream effects on lead conversion, and lead volume is influenced by too many other variables to attribute confidently to a card redesign.

4How the data was analyzed

Aggregation approach

Daily counts per arm × category are aggregated (summed) across the 13-day analysis window to get period totals. We do not average across days — the period totals are used directly in the proportion tests.

A separate cumulative trend view (running totals through each day) is also computed to check for novelty effects.

Statistical tests

  • Two-proportion z-test for rate metrics (α = 0.05, two-sided)
  • 95% confidence intervals on absolute differences (in percentage points)

What the significance labels mean

  • SIG p < 0.05 — statistically significant. The observed difference is very unlikely to be noise.
  • borderline 0.05 ≤ p < 0.10 — weak signal, not conclusive. Could be real, could be noise. Wait for more data.
  • ns p ≥ 0.10 (not significant) — no detectable difference. Treatment and control look the same within statistical noise. This is NOT proof they're identical, just that we can't tell them apart from this data.

Data scope adjustment

Excluded: 2026-06-10 → 2026-06-14 (partial rollout and ramp-up). Treatment switched on 2 days before control, so the first few days had lopsided user counts that don't reflect a clean 50/50 split. From 2026-06-15 onwards, the daily user count ratio stabilized within ~5% of 1:1 — that's our primary analysis window.

Recommendation: Continue running to the full 4-week hold, then ship if trend holds

  • Do not stop early. Even though the primary metric is decisively positive, we committed to a 4-week hold up front. Stopping early on a positive read introduces "peeking" bias and gives a less clean story to PA and stakeholders.
  • Trajectory is reliable. 13 days of stable significant lift, both verticals positive — there is no real risk in waiting the remaining ~2 weeks, and the data will only get stronger.
  • If lift holds through week 4, ship to 100% of new-Android users immediately after the hold ends.
  • Next checkpoint: 2026-07-08. Re-pull data, confirm lift hasn't decayed, verify both verticals still SIG, then make the final ship call.
  • In parallel, plan the equivalent A/B test for iOS and MWeb so we can roll out cross-platform soon after Android.
  • Monitor leads volume post-ship as a passive sanity check — but do not gate the ship decision on leads; they sit too far downstream to attribute confidently to an upper-funnel card change.
